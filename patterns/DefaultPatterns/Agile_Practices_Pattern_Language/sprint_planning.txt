SPRINT PLANNING

Before we adopted Scrum, our project never had a clear short‑term direction. We would start coding without a plan, only to realise two weeks later we were heading in the wrong direction. Then we tried sprint planning: the team and product owner together pick a set of tasks for the next two weeks.

✥ ✥ ✥

A team needs a shared understanding of what to build in the coming iteration. Without a plan, work becomes chaotic and priorities shift daily. Yet the plan must be flexible enough to accommodate learning.

Therefore:
At the beginning of each sprint, the team and product owner collaboratively select a set of product backlog items to complete. The team breaks them into tasks, estimates effort, and commits to the work. The result is a sprint backlog.

✥ ✥ ✥

Sprint planning sets the stage for DAILY STANDUP (daily_standup.txt) – the standup tracks progress against the sprint plan. It also influences RETROSPECTIVE (retrospective.txt), where the team reflects on how well the planning process worked.

This practice aligns well with TEST‑DRIVEN DEVELOPMENT (test_driven_development.txt) because planned stories can be turned into tests. The plan itself can be seen as a form of FACTORY METHOD (factory_method_demo.txt) – it creates a structured workflow.
